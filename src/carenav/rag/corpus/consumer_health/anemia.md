# Anemia

## What it is

Anemia is a condition in which the blood does not carry enough oxygen to the rest of the
body, usually because there are too few healthy red blood cells. It can develop through
blood loss, when the body makes too few red blood cells, or when red blood cells break
down too quickly. Common causes include low iron, heavy menstrual bleeding, pregnancy,
gastrointestinal problems, vitamin B12 or folic acid deficiency, and inherited disorders
such as sickle cell disease. Anemia may be mild and temporary or long-lasting and severe.

## Symptoms and signs

Many people with mild anemia have few or no symptoms, while others feel tired or weak.
Common signs include fatigue, feeling cold, dizziness, headaches, and mood changes. Some
people notice shortness of breath, a fast or irregular heartbeat, or pale skin. Symptoms
often worsen as the anemia becomes more severe.

## How it is diagnosed or evaluated

A clinician usually evaluates anemia with a physical exam and blood tests. A complete
blood count (CBC) measures the number and size of red blood cells and the amount of
hemoglobin. Further tests may check iron levels, vitamin B12, and folic acid, and in some
cases a bone marrow exam is done to find the underlying cause.

## How it is managed or treated

Treatment depends on the type and cause of the anemia. Options include iron supplements,
vitamin B12 or folic acid replacement, and dietary changes to add iron-rich foods. More
severe cases may require blood transfusions, medicines, or treatment of an underlying
condition such as bleeding or a chronic illness.

## When to seek care

See a clinician for ongoing fatigue, unexplained shortness of breath, dizziness, or pale
skin, since these can signal anemia that needs evaluation. Seek urgent care for severe or
sudden symptoms such as chest pain, fainting, or a rapid heartbeat.
