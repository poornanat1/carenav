# Heart Disease

## What it is

Heart disease is a general term for several conditions that affect the heart and its
blood vessels. The most common form is coronary artery disease, in which a sticky
substance called plaque builds up in the arteries that supply the heart muscle with
blood. The family also includes arrhythmias (irregular heartbeats), heart failure, heart
valve disease, and diseases of the heart muscle such as cardiomyopathy.

Myocardial infarction means heart attack. An acute ST-segment elevation myocardial
infarction, often called STEMI, is a type of heart attack pattern seen on an
electrocardiogram when blood flow to part of the heart muscle is blocked.

## Symptoms and signs

Symptoms vary widely depending on the type of heart disease, and some people have none at
first. Coronary disease and angina can cause chest pain or pressure, while heart failure
often brings shortness of breath, fatigue, and swelling in the legs or feet. Arrhythmias
may cause a fluttering, racing, or skipped heartbeat. In some cases the first sign is a
serious complication such as a heart attack.

## How it is diagnosed or evaluated

A clinician usually reviews personal and family medical history and performs a physical
exam. Heart and blood tests, such as electrocardiograms, echocardiograms, stress tests,
and cholesterol panels, help confirm the type and severity. People may be referred to a
cardiologist for more specialized testing and follow-up.

## How it is managed or treated

Treatment depends on the specific condition but commonly combines lifestyle changes,
medicines, and sometimes procedures or surgery. Helpful lifestyle steps include a heart-
healthy diet, regular physical activity, a healthy weight, and avoiding tobacco.
Medications and procedures may control symptoms, lower risk factors, or restore blood
flow, and cardiac rehabilitation programs support recovery and prevention.

## When to seek care

Seek emergency care for signs of a heart attack, such as chest pain or pressure, pain
spreading to the arm, jaw, or back, shortness of breath, sweating, nausea, or
lightheadedness. Call emergency services right away rather than waiting, and get prompt
care for new or worsening symptoms like fainting or a very irregular heartbeat.
