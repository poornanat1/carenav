# Chronic Kidney Disease

## What it is

Chronic kidney disease (CKD) means the kidneys are damaged and can no longer filter blood
as well as they should. The kidneys remove waste and extra fluid, help balance the body's
chemicals, regulate blood pressure, and make hormones. CKD develops slowly over time and
can worsen over months or years. Diabetes and high blood pressure are the most common
causes.

## Symptoms and signs

Many people have no symptoms until kidney disease is advanced, so it is often called a
quiet condition. As it progresses, signs can include swelling in the feet or ankles,
fatigue, trouble sleeping, changes in how much you urinate, poor appetite, and trouble
concentrating. Because early CKD usually causes no symptoms, routine screening is the
main way it is found.

## How it is diagnosed or evaluated

Blood and urine tests are the only reliable way to know whether you have kidney disease. A
blood test measures creatinine to estimate how well the kidneys filter (the eGFR), and a
urine test checks for protein, which can signal kidney damage. People with diabetes, high
blood pressure, heart disease, or a family history of kidney failure are at higher risk
and may be tested regularly.

## Stages of chronic kidney disease

CKD is divided into five stages based on the eGFR, which estimates how well the kidneys
filter blood. Stage 1 means kidney damage with normal filtering (eGFR 90 or higher), and
stage 2 means a mild decrease (eGFR 60 to 89); at these stages there are usually no
symptoms. Stage 3 is a moderate decrease (eGFR 30 to 59), when symptoms such as swelling
and fatigue may begin. Stage 4 is a severe decrease (eGFR 15 to 29), when preparing for
treatment of kidney failure is discussed. Stage 5 (eGFR below 15) is kidney failure, also
called end-stage renal disease, when dialysis or a kidney transplant is needed to live.

## How it is managed or treated

Treatment cannot reverse CKD, but it can slow its progress and protect remaining kidney
function. Care often includes medicines to control blood pressure, blood sugar, and
cholesterol, along with dietary changes such as limiting sodium and eating heart-healthy
foods. Lifestyle steps like reaching a healthy weight, staying active, not smoking, and
limiting alcohol also help. If kidneys eventually fail, dialysis or a kidney transplant
may be needed.

## When to seek care

See a clinician if you have risk factors such as diabetes or high blood pressure, or if
you notice swelling, marked fatigue, or changes in urination. Worsening symptoms or signs
of kidney failure warrant prompt medical attention.
