# Arthritis and Joint Pain

## What it is

Arthritis is a general term for any disorder that affects the joints, the places where two
bones meet such as the knee, hip, or elbow. It covers many conditions, including
osteoarthritis from aging or injury, autoimmune forms like rheumatoid and psoriatic
arthritis, gout, infectious arthritis, juvenile arthritis, and ankylosing spondylitis of
the spine. Joint pain on its own is also common and can come from these conditions or from
overuse, strains, and other causes.

## Symptoms and signs

The most common signs are pain and stiffness in the joints along with trouble moving
around. A joint may also be swollen, red, or warm, and stiffness is often worse in the
morning or after rest. Some types, especially autoimmune arthritis, can cause whole-body
symptoms such as fever, fatigue, weight loss, or skin rashes. Symptoms range from mild to
severe and often worsen with age.

## How it is diagnosed or evaluated

A clinician usually starts by asking about your medical history and symptoms and doing a
physical exam of the affected joints. Blood tests and x-rays or other imaging can help
identify the type of arthritis and rule out other causes. A specialist called a
rheumatologist may carry out further testing to confirm the diagnosis and guide treatment.

## How it is managed or treated

Treatment depends on the type of arthritis and may include medicines to relieve pain and
inflammation, slow disease, or, in some cases, surgery such as joint replacement. Self-care
measures help many people, including hot or cold packs, gentle exercise, weight management,
relaxation techniques, and the use of splints, braces, or assistive devices. The goal is to
ease pain, protect the joints, and keep you moving.

## When to seek care

See a clinician for joint pain, stiffness, or swelling that is persistent, worsening, or
limiting your movement. Get prompt care if a joint is suddenly very painful, hot, or red,
or if joint symptoms come with fever or unexplained weight loss.
