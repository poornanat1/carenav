# Stroke

## What it is

A stroke happens when blood flow to part of the brain is interrupted, so brain cells lose
the oxygen and nutrients they need and begin to die within minutes. Most strokes are
ischemic, caused by a blood clot that blocks a vessel supplying the brain. A hemorrhagic
stroke occurs when a blood vessel ruptures and bleeds into the brain. A transient ischemic
attack, or "mini-stroke," is a brief blockage that resolves but warns of higher risk.

## Symptoms and signs

Stroke symptoms come on suddenly and may include numbness or weakness of the face, arm, or
leg, often on one side of the body. Other signs are sudden confusion, trouble speaking or
understanding speech, vision problems, dizziness or loss of balance, and a severe headache
with no known cause. The F.A.S.T. check covers Face drooping, Arm weakness, Speech
difficulty, and Time to call for emergency help.

## How it is diagnosed or evaluated

A clinician reviews the symptoms and timeline and performs a physical and neurological
exam. Brain imaging such as a CT scan or MRI shows whether a stroke is ischemic or
hemorrhagic and guides treatment. Additional tests, including blood work, an EKG, and
heart imaging like echocardiography, may help find the cause, such as a clot source.

## How it is managed or treated

Treatment depends on the type of stroke and how quickly care begins. An ischemic stroke
may be treated with clot-dissolving medicine given soon after symptoms start, with blood
thinners, or with a procedure to remove the clot. A hemorrhagic stroke is managed by
controlling the bleeding, sometimes with surgery. Rehabilitation with physical, occupational,
and speech therapy helps survivors regain lost skills and function.

## When to seek care

Call 911 right away if you or someone else has any sudden stroke warning sign, even if it
goes away, because fast treatment can save brain tissue and lives. Do not wait to see if
symptoms improve on their own.
