# Upper Respiratory Infections

## What it is

Upper respiratory infections are illnesses affecting the nose, sinuses, throat, and upper
airways. The group includes the common cold, sinusitis, sore throat (pharyngitis), and
acute bronchitis. Most are caused by viruses, with rhinoviruses being the most frequent
cause of colds; more than 200 viruses can cause cold symptoms. They spread through airborne
droplets, close contact, or touching contaminated surfaces and then the face.

## Symptoms and signs

Common symptoms include sneezing, nasal congestion, runny nose, sore throat, cough, and
headache. Some people also have a mild fever, body aches, or fatigue. Symptoms usually
begin a few days after infection and often last about 10 to 14 days. The specific pattern
varies by condition, such as facial pressure with sinusitis or a lingering cough with
bronchitis.

## How it is diagnosed or evaluated

Most upper respiratory infections are diagnosed clinically based on symptoms and a physical
exam, and no specific test is required. A clinician may examine the nose, throat, and ears
and listen to the lungs. A throat swab can check for strep throat, and other tests may be
used if a more serious or bacterial infection is suspected.

## How it is managed or treated

There is no cure for most viral upper respiratory infections, but supportive care eases
symptoms while the body recovers. Helpful measures include rest, drinking fluids, using a
humidifier, gargling salt water, and saline nasal drops or sprays. Over-the-counter pain
and cold medicines may relieve symptoms, but aspirin should be avoided in children.
Antibiotics do not help viral infections and are only used when a bacterial cause is
confirmed.

## When to seek care

Contact a clinician for trouble breathing, signs of dehydration, a fever lasting more than
a few days, or symptoms that last beyond 10 days or get worse instead of better. People
with chronic lung or heart conditions, or weakened immune systems, should seek care sooner.
