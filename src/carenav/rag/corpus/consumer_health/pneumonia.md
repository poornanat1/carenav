# Pneumonia

## What it is

Pneumonia is an infection in one or both lungs that causes the tiny air sacs to fill with
fluid or pus. It can be caused by bacteria, viruses, or fungi, and it may also follow
breathing food or fluid into the lungs. Cases range from mild to severe depending on the
cause, a person's age, and overall health. Bacterial infection with Streptococcus
pneumoniae is among the most common causes.

## Symptoms and signs

Common symptoms include fever, chills, and a cough that usually brings up thick mucus
called phlegm. Many people also have shortness of breath and chest pain that worsens when
breathing or coughing. Some have nausea, vomiting, or diarrhea. Newborns may show no clear
signs, and older adults can have milder or fewer symptoms than expected.

## How it is diagnosed or evaluated

A clinician reviews symptoms and history and listens to the lungs with a stethoscope. A
chest X-ray is often used to confirm pneumonia and show how much of the lung is affected.
Blood tests, a sputum culture, or pulse oximetry may help identify the cause and gauge
severity. In severe or hospitalized cases, a CT scan or pleural fluid culture may be done.

## How it is managed or treated

Treatment depends on the cause: antibiotics treat bacterial pneumonia, antivirals may be
used for some viral cases, and antifungals treat fungal pneumonia. Rest, fluids, and
fever-reducing medicine support recovery at home for milder illness. Oxygen therapy or
hospital care may be needed if blood oxygen is low or symptoms are severe. Recovery varies
from about a week to a month or more.

## When to seek care

Seek prompt medical care for trouble breathing, chest pain, a high or persistent fever, or
confusion, and call for emergency help if breathing becomes very difficult or lips or
fingernails turn blue. Adults over 65, young children, and people with chronic conditions
or weakened immune systems face higher risk and should seek care early.
