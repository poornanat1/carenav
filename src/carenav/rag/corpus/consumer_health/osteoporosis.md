# Osteoporosis

## What it is

Osteoporosis is a disease in which bones become weak, thin, and more likely to break.
It develops when the body loses bone faster than it can replace it, lowering bone
mineral density and weakening bone structure. The condition is most common in older
adults and in women after menopause, and it raises the risk of fractures.

## Symptoms and signs

Osteoporosis is often called a "silent" disease because it usually causes no symptoms
in its early stages. Many people do not know they have it until they break a bone,
most often in the hip, spine, or wrist. Over time, weakened bones in the spine can lead
to back pain, loss of height, or a stooped posture.

## How it is diagnosed or evaluated

A bone density scan, also called a DXA test, is the main test used to measure bone
strength and diagnose osteoporosis. Providers may also review medical history, check
posture, balance, and muscle strength, and use fracture risk assessments. Screening is
commonly recommended for women age 65 and older and for younger postmenopausal women
who have added risk factors.

## How it is managed or treated

Management focuses on getting enough calcium, vitamin D, and protein and staying
physically active with weight-bearing and strength exercises. Medicines that slow bone
loss or help rebuild bone may be prescribed when fracture risk is higher. Quitting
smoking, limiting alcohol, and preventing falls at home also help protect bone health
and reduce the chance of breaks.

## When to seek care

See a healthcare provider if you have risk factors such as older age, a small body
frame, family history, or long-term use of certain medicines, so screening can be
arranged. Seek prompt medical care after any fall or injury that may have caused a
fracture, especially of the hip or spine.
