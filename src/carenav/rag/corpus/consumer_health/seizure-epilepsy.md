# Seizures and Epilepsy

## What it is

A seizure is a sudden surge of abnormal electrical activity in the brain, when clusters of
nerve cells send out the wrong signals. Epilepsy is a brain disorder in which a person has
recurring, unprovoked seizures over time. Seizures can also be triggered by other causes
such as high fever, low blood sugar, infection, head injury, or alcohol or drug withdrawal.
There are many types, ranging from brief staring spells to convulsions that affect the
whole body.

## Symptoms and signs

How a seizure looks depends on which part of the brain is involved and how far the activity
spreads. Some seizures cause violent muscle contractions, stiffening, and loss of
consciousness, while others bring only unusual sensations, emotional changes, or strange
behavior. A person may stare blankly, become confused, have jerking movements, or briefly
lose awareness. Most seizures last from a few seconds to a few minutes and stop on their own.

## How it is diagnosed or evaluated

A clinician reviews a description of the events, the person's medical history, and any
possible triggers, ideally with an account from someone who witnessed the episode. An EEG
(electroencephalogram) records the brain's electrical activity and helps identify seizure
patterns. Brain imaging such as MRI or CT, along with blood tests, is used to look for an
underlying cause; specialized or genetic testing may be added in some cases.

## How it is managed or treated

There is no cure for epilepsy, but anti-seizure medicines control seizures for most people.
When medicines do not work well, options can include epilepsy surgery, implanted devices
such as a vagus nerve stimulator, or specialized diets that help some children. Identifying
and avoiding triggers, taking medication consistently, and getting enough sleep support
control. Simple first aid, such as protecting the person from injury and turning them on
their side, helps keep them safe during a seizure.

## When to seek care

Call 911 if a seizure lasts longer than five minutes, if one seizure follows another without
recovery, or if the person does not wake up, has trouble breathing, or is injured. Seek
prompt medical care for a first-time seizure or any new change in seizure pattern.
