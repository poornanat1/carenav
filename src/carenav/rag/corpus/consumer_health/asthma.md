# Asthma

## What is asthma

Asthma is a chronic lung disease that affects the airways carrying air into and out of
your lungs. With asthma, the airways become inflamed and narrowed, causing wheezing,
coughing, and chest tightness. Symptoms can range from mild to severe and may happen daily
or only occasionally. An asthma attack, or flare-up, is when symptoms suddenly get worse.

## Triggers and symptoms

Triggers vary by person and may include allergens (dust mites, mold, pet dander, pollen),
cold air, tobacco smoke, air pollution, respiratory infections like colds and flu,
household chemicals, and exercise. Common symptoms are chest tightness, coughing
(especially at night or early morning), shortness of breath, and wheezing.

## How it is managed

Treatment combines avoiding triggers with two kinds of medicine. Quick-relief medicines,
usually an inhaler such as albuterol, ease symptoms during an attack. Long-term control
medicines, taken daily, reduce airway inflammation and help prevent attacks. A clinician
helps build an asthma action plan that explains which medicine to use and when.

## When it is an emergency

If quick-relief medicine does not control an attack, or if you have severe shortness of
breath, trouble talking, or lips or fingernails that turn blue, get emergency care right
away. A severe asthma attack can be life-threatening.
