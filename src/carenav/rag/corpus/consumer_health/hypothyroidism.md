# Hypothyroidism (Underactive Thyroid)

## What it is

Hypothyroidism, or underactive thyroid, happens when the thyroid gland does not make
enough thyroid hormone for your body's needs. The thyroid is a small, butterfly-shaped
gland in the front of your neck that helps control how your body uses energy. Without
enough thyroid hormone, your metabolism slows and many body systems are affected.

## Symptoms

Symptoms can develop slowly and vary from person to person. They may include fatigue,
weight gain, puffiness in the face, feeling cold, joint and muscle pain, constipation, dry
skin and hair, depression, and a slowed heart rate. Some people develop a goiter, an
enlarged thyroid that appears as swelling in the neck.

## How it is diagnosed

The most common cause is Hashimoto's disease, an autoimmune disorder. A clinician
diagnoses hypothyroidism using your medical history, a physical exam, and thyroid blood
tests. The TSH (thyroid-stimulating hormone) test is the standard tool, sometimes with T3,
T4, and thyroid antibody tests to confirm the diagnosis.

## How it is treated

Hypothyroidism is treated with medicine that replaces the hormone your thyroid can no
longer make, usually levothyroxine. Hormone levels are checked with blood tests, often
every six to eight weeks at first and then about once a year once the right dose is found.
Keep taking the medicine as directed, even when you feel well, and do not stop without
talking to your clinician.
