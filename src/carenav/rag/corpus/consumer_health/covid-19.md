# COVID-19

## What it is

COVID-19 is a respiratory illness caused by a virus called SARS-CoV-2. The virus spreads
mainly through respiratory droplets and small particles that infected people breathe out
when they cough, sneeze, talk, or breathe. Illness can range from no symptoms at all to
mild cold-like complaints to severe disease affecting the lungs and other organs. Some
people develop lasting problems known as post-COVID conditions, or long COVID.

## Symptoms and signs

Common symptoms include fever or chills, cough, shortness of breath, fatigue, sore throat,
congestion or runny nose, headache, and muscle or body aches. Some people also notice a new
loss of taste or smell, or nausea, vomiting, and diarrhea. Symptoms usually appear two to
fourteen days after exposure and vary widely from person to person. Older adults and people
with certain health conditions are at higher risk of becoming seriously ill.

## How it is diagnosed or evaluated

COVID-19 is confirmed with a viral test that detects the virus in a sample, usually from the
nose or throat. Rapid antigen tests can be done at home and give quick results, while
laboratory PCR tests are more sensitive. A clinician considers symptoms, recent exposures,
and test results, and may order chest imaging or blood tests when illness is more severe.

## How it is managed or treated

Many people recover at home with rest, fluids, and over-the-counter medicine to ease fever
and aches. Antiviral medicines may be prescribed for people at higher risk of severe illness
and work best when started early after symptoms begin. Staying up to date with recommended
vaccines, washing hands, improving ventilation, and wearing a mask when appropriate help
prevent infection and reduce spread to others.

## When to seek care

Get emergency care for trouble breathing, persistent chest pain or pressure, new confusion,
trouble staying awake, or bluish lips or face. People at higher risk should contact a
clinician early to ask whether treatment is right for them.
