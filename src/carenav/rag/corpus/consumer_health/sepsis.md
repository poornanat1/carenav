# Sepsis

## What it is

Sepsis is the body's overactive and extreme response to an infection, in which the immune
reaction damages its own tissues and organs. It is a life-threatening medical emergency
that can progress quickly to organ failure and death without prompt treatment. Any
infection can trigger sepsis, including infections of the lungs, urinary tract, skin, or
gut. Some people face higher risk, including adults over 65, infants, pregnant women, and
those with chronic conditions or weakened immune systems.

## Symptoms and signs

Common signs include a rapid heart rate and rapid breathing, shortness of breath, and a
fever with chills or feeling very cold. Many people develop confusion or disorientation,
extreme pain or discomfort, and clammy or sweaty skin. Symptoms can come on quickly and
may differ from one person to the next. Sepsis often appears alongside an existing
infection that is worsening rather than improving.

## How it is diagnosed or evaluated

A clinician reviews the person's medical history and performs a physical exam that includes
checking vital signs such as temperature, heart rate, and breathing. Laboratory tests look
for markers of infection and signs of organ damage. Imaging studies such as X-rays or CT
scans may be used to locate the underlying source of infection. Rapid evaluation matters
because early recognition improves outcomes.

## How it is managed or treated

Treatment usually begins quickly with antibiotics to fight the infection and intravenous
fluids and oxygen to maintain blood flow to the organs. Medicines to raise blood pressure
may be given when needed, and the source of infection is treated directly. Severe cases
may require care in a hospital or intensive care unit. Some people need dialysis, a
breathing machine, or surgery to remove infected or damaged tissue.

## When to seek care

Seek emergency medical care right away if you suspect sepsis or notice an infection that is
getting worse or not improving. Signs such as rapid breathing, confusion, extreme pain,
fever with chills, or clammy skin need urgent evaluation. People at higher risk should act
early, because sepsis can worsen rapidly.
