# COPD (Chronic Obstructive Pulmonary Disease)

## What it is

COPD is a group of chronic lung diseases that block airflow and make breathing difficult.
It includes conditions such as emphysema and chronic bronchitis. Over time the airways lose
elasticity, air sac walls are damaged, airway walls become inflamed and thickened, and the
airways make extra mucus, so less air flows in and out. Smoking is the leading cause, and
the disease usually develops slowly and worsens over years.

## Symptoms and signs

Early COPD may cause few or no symptoms. As it progresses, common signs include a frequent
cough that often brings up mucus, wheezing, chest tightness, and shortness of breath that
is worse with activity. More severe disease can lead to frequent respiratory infections,
fatigue, unintended weight loss, muscle weakness, and swelling in the ankles or legs.

## How it is diagnosed or evaluated

A clinician reviews your symptoms, medical and family history, and exposures such as smoking.
Lung function tests, including spirometry that measures how much and how fast you can breathe
out, are used to confirm COPD and gauge its severity. A chest x-ray or CT scan and blood
tests may also be done to assess the lungs and oxygen levels and to rule out other causes.

## How it is managed or treated

There is no cure, but treatment can ease symptoms and slow progression. Quitting smoking is
the single most important step. Care may include inhaled bronchodilators and other medicines,
flu and pneumonia vaccines to prevent infections, pulmonary rehabilitation, and supplemental
oxygen for those with low blood oxygen. A clinician helps tailor a plan to your needs.

## When to seek care

Get emergency care if you cannot catch your breath, your lips or fingernails turn blue, or
you have trouble talking or thinking clearly. Contact your provider if symptoms worsen or you
notice signs of a lung infection, such as fever or a change in mucus.
