# Sleep Apnea

## What it is

Sleep apnea is a common sleep disorder in which breathing repeatedly stops or becomes very
shallow during sleep. These pauses can happen many times an hour and last from a few
seconds to minutes. The most common form is obstructive sleep apnea, where the upper airway
collapses or becomes blocked; central sleep apnea, a less common type, occurs when the brain
does not properly signal the muscles that control breathing.

## Symptoms and signs

A common sign is loud, ongoing snoring, often interrupted by pauses, snorts, or choking
sounds, though not everyone who snores has sleep apnea. Many people feel very drowsy or fall
asleep during the day, and may have morning headaches, trouble concentrating, or irritability.
Risk is higher in people who are overweight, are male, have a family history, or have small
airways; in children it is often linked to enlarged tonsils or adenoids.

## How it is diagnosed or evaluated

Diagnosis is based on medical and family history, a physical exam, and the results of a
sleep study. A sleep study records breathing, oxygen levels, heart rate, and other signals,
either in a sleep lab or with a home testing device. These results help confirm the diagnosis
and show how severe the apnea is.

## How it is managed or treated

Treatment depends on the type and severity and may combine several approaches. Lifestyle
changes such as losing weight, avoiding alcohol, and changing sleep position can help milder
cases. Breathing devices like CPAP, which delivers steady air pressure to keep the airway
open, are a common treatment, and oral mouthpieces or surgery may be used in some cases.

## When to seek care

See a clinician if you have loud snoring with breathing pauses, choking during sleep, or
strong daytime sleepiness, since untreated sleep apnea raises the risk of car crashes,
work accidents, and other health problems. Treatment is important when these symptoms are
present.
