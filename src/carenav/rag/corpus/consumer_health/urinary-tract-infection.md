# Urinary Tract Infections

## What it is

A urinary tract infection (UTI) is an infection in any part of the urinary system, which
includes the kidneys, ureters, bladder, and urethra. UTIs are among the most common
infections in the body and are usually caused by bacteria. They are named for the part of
the tract affected: cystitis is a bladder infection, urethritis is a urethra infection,
and pyelonephritis is a kidney infection. Women develop UTIs much more often than men.

## Symptoms and signs

Common symptoms include a burning feeling when urinating, a frequent or strong urge to
urinate, and passing only small amounts of urine. The urine may look cloudy, reddish, or
have a strong odor, and there may be pressure or discomfort in the lower abdomen. Kidney
infections are more serious and can cause fever, chills, fatigue, and pain in the back or
side below the ribs.

## How it is diagnosed or evaluated

A UTI is usually confirmed with a urine test that checks for bacteria, white blood cells,
or other signs of infection. A urine culture can identify the specific bacteria and guide
treatment. For repeated or complicated infections, a clinician may order imaging of the
urinary tract or a cystoscopy to look inside the bladder and urethra.

## How it is managed or treated

UTIs are typically treated with antibiotics chosen to match the bacteria causing the
infection. Drinking plenty of fluids and finishing the full course of medicine as
prescribed help clear the infection. Some people can lower their risk by staying hydrated,
urinating when needed, and good hygiene; those with catheters, diabetes, or other risk
factors may need extra care.

## When to seek care

If you think you have a UTI, see a clinician for evaluation and treatment, since untreated
infections can spread to the kidneys. Seek prompt medical care for fever, chills, back or
side pain, vomiting, or blood in the urine, which may signal a kidney infection.
