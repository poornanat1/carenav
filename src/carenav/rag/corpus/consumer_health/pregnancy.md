# Pregnancy

## What it is

Pregnancy is the period during which a fetus develops inside the uterus, usually lasting
about 40 weeks from the last menstrual period until birth. It is divided into three
trimesters, and the body changes continuously as the fetus grows. A full-term pregnancy
spans roughly nine months.

## Symptoms and signs

A missed menstrual period is often the first sign of pregnancy. Early symptoms can include
nausea and vomiting (often called morning sickness), breast tenderness, fatigue, and
frequent urination. As pregnancy progresses, common changes include weight gain, back
pain, skin changes, stretch marks, and disrupted sleep. Symptoms vary widely from person
to person and across trimesters.

## How it is diagnosed or evaluated

Pregnancy is commonly confirmed with a urine test that detects the hormone human chorionic
gonadotropin (hCG), available as home test kits or in a clinic. A blood test can also
measure hCG and confirm pregnancy. Once confirmed, regular prenatal care visits monitor
the health of the pregnant person and the developing fetus, often using ultrasound and
routine lab tests to track progress.

## How it is managed or treated

Ongoing prenatal care is central to a healthy pregnancy, allowing clinicians to find and
treat problems early. Good nutrition includes a variety of fruits, vegetables, whole
grains, lean protein, and low-fat dairy, along with prenatal vitamins containing folic
acid and iron. Regular physical activity, as advised by a provider, helps maintain
strength and prepare the body for birth. Avoiding alcohol, tobacco, and drugs is important
because these can harm the developing fetus.

## When to seek care

Contact a healthcare provider about any symptom that is worrying, such as vaginal
bleeding, severe abdominal pain, severe headache, or reduced fetal movement. Seek urgent
or emergency care for heavy bleeding, fluid leaking from the vagina, severe swelling, or
signs of preterm labor.
