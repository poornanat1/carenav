# Migraine and Headache

## What it is

Headache is pain or discomfort in the head, scalp, or neck, and it is one of the most
common health complaints. Migraine is a specific recurring type of headache that causes
moderate to severe throbbing or pulsing pain, often on one side of the head. Common forms
include tension-type headache, migraine, and cluster headache, and some headaches are
secondary to another condition. Migraine variants include migraine with aura, ocular
migraine, abdominal migraine in children, and familial hemiplegic migraine.

## Symptoms and signs

Migraine pain is frequently accompanied by nausea, vomiting, and increased sensitivity to
light, sound, and odors. Some people notice warning signs before an attack, such as food
cravings, mood changes, yawning, or visual disturbances like flashing lights or zig-zag
patterns called aura. Attacks can last from hours to days, and exhaustion or confusion may
linger afterward. Tension-type headaches tend to cause a dull, pressing pain on both sides
of the head.

## How it is diagnosed or evaluated

A clinician usually diagnoses headache and migraine by taking a medical history and asking
about the pattern, triggers, and timing of symptoms. Physical and neurological exams help
assess the cause. An important part of evaluation is ruling out other medical conditions,
so blood tests or imaging such as MRI or CT scans may be ordered when a more serious cause
is suspected.

## How it is managed or treated

There is no cure for migraine, but many treatments can reduce how often attacks occur and
ease symptoms when they happen. Acute options include pain relievers and migraine-specific
drugs such as triptans or ergotamines, often most effective when taken early. Resting in a
dark, quiet room and applying cool compresses can help during an attack. Long-term measures
include identifying and avoiding triggers, managing stress, healthy lifestyle habits, and
preventive medications for frequent or severe headaches.

## When to seek care

Seek emergency care for a sudden, severe headache that feels like the worst of your life,
or a headache with fever, stiff neck, confusion, weakness, vision loss, trouble speaking,
or that follows a head injury. Contact a clinician for new, worsening, or unusually frequent
headaches, or headaches that do not respond to usual treatment.
