# Type 2 Diabetes

## Overview

Type 2 diabetes is a condition in which the body does not use insulin properly. This
is called insulin resistance. At first, the pancreas makes extra insulin to make up for
it. Over time it cannot make enough to keep blood glucose at normal levels. Type 2
diabetes is the most common form of diabetes and is most often diagnosed in adults,
though it is increasingly seen in children and teens.

## Symptoms

Symptoms of type 2 diabetes often develop slowly over several years and can be mild
enough to go unnoticed. Common symptoms include increased thirst, frequent urination,
increased hunger, unintended weight loss, fatigue, blurred vision, slow-healing sores,
and frequent infections. Some people have no symptoms at all. Because symptoms are easy
to miss, blood-glucose screening is recommended for adults with risk factors.

## Managing your condition

Managing type 2 diabetes focuses on keeping blood glucose in a target range. This
usually means a combination of healthy eating, regular physical activity, maintaining a
healthy weight, monitoring blood glucose, and — when needed — medicine such as
metformin or insulin. Working with a care team to set an individualized A1C goal is
important. Managing blood pressure and cholesterol lowers the risk of heart and kidney
complications.

## When to seek care

Seek medical care promptly for very high blood glucose with symptoms such as extreme
thirst, confusion, or fruity-smelling breath, which can signal a medical emergency.
Signs of very low blood glucose — shakiness, sweating, confusion, or loss of
consciousness — also need prompt treatment. These descriptions are general health
information and are not a substitute for advice from a licensed clinician.
