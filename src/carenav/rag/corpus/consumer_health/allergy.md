# Allergies

## What it is

An allergy is a reaction by the immune system to a substance that does not bother most
other people. The immune system mistakenly treats a harmless substance, called an allergen,
as a threat and releases chemicals that cause symptoms. Both genetics and environment play
a role in who develops allergies. Common allergens include pollen, dust mites, mold spores,
pet dander, certain foods, insect stings, and medicines.

## Symptoms and signs

Allergic reactions vary widely depending on the trigger and the person. Common signs
include a runny or stuffy nose, sneezing, itching, watery eyes, rashes or hives, and
swelling. Some allergies can trigger or worsen asthma symptoms such as wheezing and
shortness of breath. Severity ranges from mild and annoying to serious and, rarely,
life-threatening.

## How it is diagnosed or evaluated

A clinician reviews symptoms, their timing, and possible exposures to identify likely
triggers. Skin tests and blood tests are used to help confirm which allergens a person
reacts to. Keeping track of when and where symptoms occur can help pinpoint the cause.

## How it is managed or treated

The most effective step is avoiding known allergens whenever possible. Medicines such as
antihistamines, nasal sprays, and decongestants can relieve symptoms. For some people,
allergy shots (immunotherapy) reduce sensitivity to a trigger over time. Nasal washes and
other self-care measures may also ease nasal and sinus symptoms.

## When to seek care

See a clinician if you suspect allergies or if symptoms are persistent, severe, or not
helped by usual treatment. Anaphylaxis is a severe, rapid allergic reaction that can cause
trouble breathing, throat swelling, or a drop in blood pressure and is a medical emergency;
call for emergency help right away.
