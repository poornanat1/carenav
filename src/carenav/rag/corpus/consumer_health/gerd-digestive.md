# GERD and Digestive Disorders

## What it is

Gastroesophageal reflux disease (GERD) is a common digestive disorder in which stomach acid
repeatedly flows back up into the esophagus. It happens when the muscle at the lower end of
the esophagus does not close properly, allowing acid reflux. Occasional reflux is normal,
but GERD is diagnosed when reflux occurs two or more times a week or damages the esophagus.
It belongs to a broader family of acid-related and digestive conditions that affect the
esophagus, stomach, and gastrointestinal tract.

## Symptoms and signs

The most common symptom is heartburn, a burning feeling in the chest or throat. Many people
also notice an acid or food taste at the back of the mouth, especially after meals or when
lying down. Other signs can include a dry cough, hoarse voice, asthma-like symptoms, and
difficulty swallowing. Symptoms often worsen after large meals, certain trigger foods, or
bending over.

## How it is diagnosed or evaluated

Clinicians usually start by reviewing symptoms and medical history, and many cases are
managed based on the clinical picture alone. When further evaluation is needed, an upper GI
endoscopy can look directly at the esophagus and stomach, sometimes with a biopsy. Other
tests include esophageal pH monitoring, esophageal manometry, X-rays, and transnasal
esophagoscopy to assess reflux and rule out other causes.

## How it is managed or treated

Treatment combines lifestyle changes with medicines as needed. Helpful measures include
eating smaller meals, avoiding lying down for two to three hours after eating, limiting
trigger foods such as spicy, fatty, chocolate, and caffeinated items, losing excess weight,
quitting smoking, and raising the head of the bed. Over-the-counter antacids and prescription
acid-reducing medicines relieve symptoms and help the esophagus heal. Surgery is rarely
needed and is reserved for cases that do not respond to other treatment.

## When to seek care

Seek care if symptoms are frequent, persist despite treatment, or include trouble swallowing,
unexplained weight loss, or vomiting. Get emergency help for chest pain with shortness of
breath or pain spreading to the jaw or arm, which can signal a heart problem rather than
reflux.
