# High Cholesterol

## What is high cholesterol

Cholesterol is a waxy substance found in all of your body's cells. Your body makes the
cholesterol it needs, and you also get it from animal-based foods such as meat, eggs, and
cheese. When the level of cholesterol in your blood is too high, it can combine with other
substances to form plaque that builds up in the walls of your arteries, a process called
atherosclerosis. High cholesterol usually has no symptoms.

Hypertriglyceridemia means the level of triglycerides, a type of fat in the blood, is too
high. Triglycerides are measured on a lipid panel along with LDL and HDL cholesterol.

## Why it matters

As plaque builds up, it can narrow or block the coronary arteries, which can cause angina
(chest pain) and heart attack. Plaque can build up in other arteries too, raising the risk
of stroke and peripheral arterial disease. Because high cholesterol has no symptoms,
undiagnosed or untreated high cholesterol can lead to serious cardiovascular events.

## How it is diagnosed

A blood test measures your cholesterol and lipid levels, including LDL ("bad"
cholesterol), HDL ("good" cholesterol), and triglycerides. How often you are tested
depends on your age and risk factors; many adults are checked every four to six years, and
more often if they have risk factors. A clinician interprets the results in the context of
your overall heart-disease risk.

## How it is managed

The first step is usually heart-healthy lifestyle changes: a diet low in saturated fat,
regular physical activity, reaching and keeping a healthy weight, and avoiding tobacco. If
lifestyle changes are not enough, a clinician may prescribe a cholesterol-lowering
medicine such as a statin. Take medicines as directed and keep follow-up lab appointments.
