# Preventive vs. Diagnostic Care — Coverage Basics

This explainer covers a standard health-coverage concept using definitions consistent with
the federal Uniform Glossary that accompanies the Summary of Benefits and Coverage (SBC).
It is general coverage information for the CareNav demo, not personal benefits advice.

## Preventive care

Preventive care is routine health care that includes screenings, check-ups, and counseling
to prevent illness, disease, or other health problems. Many recommended preventive services
are covered at no cost to you—no copay or coinsurance—when you receive them from an
in-network provider, even if you have not yet met your deductible. Examples include
many recommended screenings, immunizations, and well visits.

## Diagnostic care

Diagnostic care is testing or services done to investigate symptoms or follow up on a
finding—for example, a lab test or imaging study ordered because you have a specific
complaint. Diagnostic services are generally subject to your plan's normal cost sharing,
such as the deductible and coinsurance, rather than the no-cost preventive benefit.

## How the same test can be billed either way

The same service can count as preventive or diagnostic depending on why it is done. A
screening test performed on a healthy person with no symptoms is usually preventive, while
the same test ordered to evaluate a symptom or an abnormal result is usually diagnostic and
may cost more. The clinician's reason for ordering the service, recorded with a diagnosis
code, drives how it is covered.

## What to do

If you are unsure whether a visit or test will be covered as preventive, ask your provider
how it will be coded and check your plan documents. Refer to the full policy for the list
of covered preventive services and the cost sharing that applies to diagnostic services.
