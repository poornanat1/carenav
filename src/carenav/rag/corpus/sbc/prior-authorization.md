# Prior Authorization — Coverage Basics

This explainer covers a standard health-coverage concept using definitions consistent with
the federal Uniform Glossary that accompanies the Summary of Benefits and Coverage (SBC).
It is general coverage information for the CareNav demo, not personal benefits advice.

## What prior authorization is

Prior authorization—also called preauthorization or prior approval—is a decision by your
health plan that a service, treatment plan, prescription drug, or piece of equipment is
medically necessary. Your plan may require you (or your provider) to get this approval
before you receive the service, except in an emergency.

## What it does and does not mean

Prior authorization is not a promise that your plan will cover the cost. Even when a
service is preauthorized, coverage still depends on your eligibility, your benefits, and
the plan's rules, and you may still owe cost sharing such as a copay or coinsurance. If a
service that requires prior authorization is done without it, the plan may reduce or deny
the benefit.

## Services that often require it

Plans commonly require prior authorization for advanced imaging such as MRI and CT scans,
non-emergency hospital stays, certain procedures, and some high-cost or specialty
prescription drugs. Routine primary care visits and most preventive services generally do
not require prior authorization.

## What to do

If a service may need prior authorization, your provider's office usually submits the
request to the plan. You can confirm whether a specific service or drug requires approval by
checking your plan documents or contacting your plan before the service. Refer to the full
policy for the complete list of services that require prior authorization.
