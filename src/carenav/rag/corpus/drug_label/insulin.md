# Insulin

## Indications and usage

Insulin and other injectable diabetes medicines are used to control blood sugar in people
with diabetes mellitus. Insulin helps move sugar from the blood into body tissues where it
is used for energy and limits excess glucose production by the liver. Insulin is required
for type 1 diabetes and is also used in type 2 diabetes when other treatments do not
provide adequate control. Some injectable medicines such as liraglutide are used with diet
and exercise to improve glucose control in type 2 diabetes.

## Dosage and administration

These medicines are injected under the skin (subcutaneously); insulin is often given one or
more times each day, and doses are individualized by the prescriber. They are supplied in
vials, prefilled pens, and cartridges, and only the prescribed product type and
concentration should be used. Inject into the stomach, upper arm, thigh, or buttocks,
rotate injection sites, and never reuse needles or share supplies. Do not change the dose
or stop the medicine without talking to your clinician.

## Warnings and precautions

Tell your clinician about any allergy to the medicine and about heart, kidney, liver,
nerve, or endocrine conditions before starting. Doses may need adjustment during illness,
stress, travel, surgery, pregnancy, or changes in diet and exercise. Alcohol and certain
medicines, including some that affect blood sugar, can change how these medicines work, so
review all medicines with your clinician or pharmacist. Blood sugar should be monitored as
directed to avoid levels that are too low or too high.

## Adverse reactions

Common side effects include redness, swelling, or skin changes at the injection site,
weight changes, and gastrointestinal effects such as nausea or constipation. The most
important risk is low blood sugar (hypoglycemia), which can cause sweating, shakiness,
dizziness, confusion, and, if severe, seizures or loss of consciousness. Seek immediate
care for signs of a serious allergic reaction such as rash, itching, shortness of breath,
wheezing, fast or abnormal heartbeat, or swelling.
