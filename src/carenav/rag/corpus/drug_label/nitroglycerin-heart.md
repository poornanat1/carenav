# Nitroglycerin and Heart Medications

## Indications and usage

Nitroglycerin is a vasodilator used to treat episodes of angina (chest pain) in people with
coronary artery disease. It relaxes and widens blood vessels so the heart needs less oxygen
and does less work. The sublingual (under-the-tongue) form is taken to relieve an angina
attack or to prevent one before activities that are likely to trigger chest pain.

## Dosage and administration

The sublingual tablet is placed under the tongue or between the cheek and gum and allowed to
dissolve; it should not be chewed, crushed, or swallowed. It is used at the first sign of an
angina attack, or 5 to 10 minutes before activity that may bring one on. If pain continues, a
second and then a third dose may be taken 5 minutes apart, and emergency care should be sought
if chest pain remains after the third dose. A mild burning or tingling in the mouth is normal.

## Warnings and precautions

People with a recent heart attack, anemia, or increased pressure in the head should tell their
clinician before use. Nitroglycerin must not be combined with erectile-dysfunction or pulmonary
hypertension drugs such as sildenafil, tadalafil, or vardenafil, because the combination can
cause a dangerous drop in blood pressure. It commonly causes dizziness and lightheadedness, so
rise slowly; alcohol and dry-mouth medicines can worsen effects, and tolerance may develop with
frequent use.

## Adverse reactions

Common side effects include headache, flushing, and dizziness or lightheadedness. Seek prompt
medical care for blurred vision, rash, hives, trouble breathing or swallowing, fainting, a slow
or pounding heartbeat, severe or persistent headache, or chest pain that does not ease after
dosing.
