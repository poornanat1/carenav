# Immunosuppressants (Transplant Medications)

## Indications and usage

Immunosuppressants are medicines that lower the activity of the immune system to keep it
from attacking transplanted organs. They are used together with other medicines to prevent
and treat rejection in people who have received a kidney, liver, heart, or other organ
transplant. Common examples include tacrolimus and cyclosporine, which are usually taken
for as long as the transplanted organ is in place.

## Dosage and administration

These medicines come in capsules, tablets, liquids, and injections, and the dose is set by
the transplant team and adjusted based on blood test results. Oral forms such as tacrolimus
are usually taken by mouth twice a day at the same times each day, often on a consistent
schedule with regard to food, and capsules should be swallowed whole and not crushed or
chewed. Do not switch between different brands or formulations without your clinician's
guidance, and do not stop taking the medicine or change the dose on your own.

## Warnings and precautions

Tell your clinician about kidney, liver, or heart disease, high blood pressure, diabetes,
high potassium, infections, or any history of cancer before starting treatment. Because
these medicines weaken the immune system, they raise the risk of serious infections and of
certain cancers such as skin cancer and lymphoma, so avoid people who are sick and limit sun
exposure. Many drugs, foods, and supplements can change blood levels, including grapefruit,
certain antibiotics and antifungals, and St. John's wort, so report all medicines you take
and avoid live vaccines unless approved.

## Adverse reactions

Common side effects include headache, tremor or shaking, trouble sleeping, nausea, diarrhea,
high blood pressure, and tingling in the hands or feet. Get medical attention right away for
signs of infection such as fever or sore throat, decreased urination or swelling, severe
stomach pain, confusion, seizures, unusual bleeding or bruising, irregular heartbeat, or an
allergic reaction such as rash, hives, or swelling of the face or throat.
