# Opioid Pain Medications

## Indications and usage

Opioid pain medicines are prescription drugs used to relieve moderate to severe pain, such
as pain after surgery or injury, cancer pain, and some chronic pain conditions. Common
examples include oxycodone, hydrocodone, codeine, morphine, fentanyl, and tramadol, and
several of these are sold combined with acetaminophen. They work by acting on opioid
receptors in the brain and body to reduce the sensation of pain.

## Dosage and administration

These medicines are taken exactly as prescribed, by mouth as tablets, capsules, or liquid,
or by other routes such as patches or injection depending on the product. The lowest
effective dose for the shortest needed time is generally used, and the dose and schedule
are set by the prescriber. Do not increase the dose, take it more often, or use someone
else's medicine, and do not stop a long-term opioid suddenly without medical guidance.

## Warnings and precautions

Opioids carry a serious risk of misuse, addiction (opioid use disorder), and life-threatening
overdose, and the risk rises when they are taken in higher doses or not as directed.
Combining opioids with alcohol, benzodiazepines, sleep medicines, or other sedating drugs
can dangerously slow breathing. Use during pregnancy can cause neonatal opioid withdrawal in
the newborn, and having naloxone available is advised for people at higher risk of overdose.

## Adverse reactions

Common side effects include drowsiness, mental fogginess, nausea, and constipation. Slowed
or shallow breathing, very small pupils, extreme sleepiness or loss of consciousness, limp
body, or pale, blue, or cold skin can signal a life-threatening overdose and require
emergency care and naloxone right away.
