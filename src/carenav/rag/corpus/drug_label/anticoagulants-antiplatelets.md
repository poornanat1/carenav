# Blood Thinners (Anticoagulants and Antiplatelets)

## Indications and usage

Blood thinners help prevent harmful blood clots from forming or growing in the heart and
blood vessels. Anticoagulants such as warfarin, heparin, and enoxaparin slow the body's
clotting process and are used for conditions such as atrial fibrillation, heart valve
replacement, and to prevent clots after surgery or in the legs and lungs. Antiplatelets
such as aspirin, clopidogrel, prasugrel, and ticagrelor keep platelets from clumping
together and are mainly used by people who have had a heart attack or stroke.

## Dosage and administration

These medicines are given in different ways depending on the drug: some, such as warfarin,
aspirin, clopidogrel, prasugrel, and ticagrelor, are taken by mouth, while heparin and
enoxaparin are given by injection or infusion. The dose is set by the prescriber and may
be adjusted over time. Warfarin requires regular blood tests so the dose can be kept in a
safe range, and it should be taken at about the same time each day. Do not start, stop, or
change the dose without talking to your clinician.

## Warnings and precautions

The main risk with all blood thinners is bleeding, so they must be taken exactly as
directed. They may interact with many foods, other medicines, vitamins, herbal products,
and alcohol, so tell every healthcare provider and pharmacist about everything you take.
Tell providers before any surgery, dental work, or procedure, and seek care for any fall
or head injury. People who are pregnant or planning pregnancy should discuss risks, as
some blood thinners can harm a developing baby.

## Adverse reactions

Bleeding is the most common side effect, and milder effects can include upset stomach,
nausea, and diarrhea. Get medical care right away for signs of serious bleeding such as
red or brown urine, black or bloody stools, vomiting or coughing up blood, severe or
unusual headache, unusual bruising, heavy menstrual bleeding, or bleeding that will not
stop.
