# Dementia Medications

## Indications and usage

Dementia medications are used to treat the symptoms of dementia in people with Alzheimer's
disease and, in some cases, other forms of dementia. This class includes cholinesterase
inhibitors such as donepezil and galantamine, as well as memantine, which acts on a
different brain chemical. These medicines may improve memory, attention, and the ability to
think and interact with others, or help slow the worsening of symptoms. They manage symptoms
rather than cure the underlying disease.

## Dosage and administration

These medicines are usually taken by mouth and are available as tablets, orally
disintegrating tablets, extended-release forms, or liquid, depending on the drug. Donepezil
is often taken once daily in the evening, while galantamine and memantine may be taken once
or twice a day with food; a clinician usually starts a low dose and increases it gradually
over several weeks. Take the exact dose prescribed at about the same time each day, and do
not crush or split tablets unless told it is safe to do so. Keep taking the medicine as
directed even if you do not notice an immediate change, and have follow-up visits so the dose
can be reviewed.

## Warnings and precautions

Tell your clinician if you are allergic to the medicine or related drugs, and report any
history of stomach ulcers or bleeding, irregular or slow heartbeat, seizures, asthma or COPD,
trouble urinating, or kidney, liver, or heart disease. Cholinesterase inhibitors such as
donepezil and galantamine can slow the heart rate, so caution is needed in people with
certain heart-rhythm conditions. Tell your clinician about all prescription and nonprescription
medicines and supplements you take, and inform any surgeon or dentist that you take this
medicine before a procedure. Discuss pregnancy, breastfeeding, and any planned surgery with
your clinician before starting or stopping treatment.

## Adverse reactions

Common side effects may include nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, loss of appetite, weight loss,
muscle cramps, headache, dizziness, and trouble sleeping. Get medical care right away for
fainting, a slow or irregular heartbeat, chest pain, trouble breathing, severe stomach pain,
blood in the stool or vomit, seizures, or unusual bruising or bleeding. Seek emergency help
for signs of an allergic reaction such as rash, swelling, or difficulty breathing.
