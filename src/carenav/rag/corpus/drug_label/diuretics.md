# Diuretics (Water Pills)

## Indications and usage

Diuretics, often called water pills, help the body remove extra salt and water through the
urine. They are used to treat high blood pressure (hypertension) and to reduce fluid buildup
(edema) caused by heart failure, kidney disease, or liver disease. Common members include the
thiazide diuretic hydrochlorothiazide and the loop diuretic furosemide. They may be used alone
or together with other medicines.

## Dosage and administration

Diuretics are usually taken by mouth, often once daily, with the dose and timing set by the
prescriber based on the condition and response. They are typically taken in the morning so that
increased urination does not interrupt sleep. Hydrochlorothiazide is generally taken once a day,
while furosemide may be taken one or more times a day. Take it at about the same time each day
and do not stop without talking to your clinician, even if you feel well.

## Warnings and precautions

Diuretics can lower blood levels of potassium, sodium, and other electrolytes, which are checked
with blood tests; loop diuretics such as furosemide can cause larger fluid and electrolyte
losses. Tell your prescriber about kidney disease, diabetes, gout, or a sulfa allergy, and about
all other medicines, since diuretics interact with blood pressure drugs, lithium, NSAIDs, and
digoxin. They can cause dizziness or lightheadedness, especially when standing up or when fluid
loss is too rapid. Use cautiously in older adults and during pregnancy.

## Adverse reactions

Common side effects include increased urination, dizziness, headache, and muscle cramps. Get
medical care for signs of low potassium or dehydration such as severe weakness, irregular
heartbeat, confusion, or fainting, and for a serious allergic reaction with rash, swelling, or
trouble breathing.
