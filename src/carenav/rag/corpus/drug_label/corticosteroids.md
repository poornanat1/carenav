# Corticosteroids (Steroids)

## Indications and usage

Corticosteroids are medicines that reduce inflammation and calm an overactive immune
system. They are used to treat conditions such as arthritis, severe allergic reactions,
asthma, lupus, multiple sclerosis, and inflammatory disorders of the skin, lungs, eyes,
kidneys, blood, and intestines, as well as to replace low natural corticosteroid levels.
Common examples include oral prednisone, taken by mouth for systemic conditions, and
topical hydrocortisone, applied to the skin for rashes, itching, and irritation.

## Dosage and administration

Corticosteroids come in many forms, including tablets, liquids, creams, and injections,
and the dose depends on the condition being treated and is often adjusted during treatment.
Oral forms such as prednisone are usually taken with food, one to four times a day or every
other day, and delayed-release tablets should be swallowed whole. Topical forms such as
hydrocortisone are applied in a thin layer to the affected skin as directed. Do not stop a
systemic corticosteroid abruptly without talking to your clinician, as the dose may need to
be lowered gradually.

## Warnings and precautions

Tell your clinician about diabetes, high blood pressure, osteoporosis, glaucoma or eye
infections, seizures, stomach ulcers, tuberculosis, mental illness, or liver, kidney, or
heart disease before starting treatment. Corticosteroids can lower your ability to fight
infection, so avoid people with chickenpox or measles and do not get vaccinations without
your clinician's approval. Some medicines and supplements can interact, including aspirin,
cimetidine, and St. John's wort, so report all medicines you take.

## Adverse reactions

Common side effects include headache, dizziness, mood changes, trouble sleeping, increased
appetite, acne, heartburn, and increased sweating; topical forms may cause burning, itching,
or skin irritation where applied. Get medical attention right away for vision problems,
signs of infection such as fever, seizures, irregular heartbeat, sudden weight gain or
swelling, depression, difficulty breathing, or an allergic reaction such as rash, hives, or
swelling of the face or throat.
