# Hormonal Birth Control

## Indications and usage

Hormonal contraceptives are used to prevent pregnancy by supplying the hormones estrogen and
progestin, or progestin alone, to suppress ovulation, thicken cervical mucus, and thin the
uterine lining. The class includes combined estrogen/progestin pills, progestin-only pills,
intrauterine devices (IUDs), and implants, using ingredients such as ethinyl estradiol,
levonorgestrel, norethindrone, and etonogestrel. Some products are also prescribed to regulate
menstrual cycles, reduce heavy or painful periods, or treat acne and other conditions.

## Dosage and administration

Combined and progestin-only pills are taken by mouth once daily, ideally at the same time each
day, and progestin-only pills are especially sensitive to being taken late. Long-acting methods
are placed by a clinician: implants are thin rods inserted under the skin of the upper arm and
last several years, while hormonal IUDs are placed in the uterus and last from three to several
years. A missed pill should be handled according to the package instructions, and a backup
method such as condoms may be needed until protection is restored.

## Warnings and precautions

People with a history of blood clots, stroke, certain heart conditions, uncontrolled high blood
pressure, breast cancer, liver disease, or migraine with aura should tell their clinician, since
estrogen-containing methods raise the risk of dangerous clots. Smoking, especially after age 35,
further increases this risk with combined products. Some medicines, including certain seizure
drugs, the antibiotic rifampin, and the herb St. John's wort, can make hormonal contraceptives
less effective. Hormonal birth control does not protect against HIV or other sexually
transmitted infections.

## Adverse reactions

Common side effects include nausea, breast tenderness, headache, mood changes, weight changes,
and irregular bleeding or spotting, which often ease over the first few months. Seek prompt
medical care for warning signs of a serious problem, such as severe leg pain or swelling, chest
pain, trouble breathing, sudden severe headache, vision changes, weakness or numbness on one
side, or severe abdominal pain.
