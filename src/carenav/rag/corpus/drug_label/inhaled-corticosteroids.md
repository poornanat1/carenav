# Inhaled Corticosteroids

## Indications and usage

Inhaled corticosteroids are used to control and prevent the symptoms of asthma and, in some
cases, chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD), such as wheezing, shortness of breath,
coughing, and chest tightness. Common members of this class include fluticasone and
budesonide, which are sometimes combined with a long-acting beta-agonist such as salmeterol
in a single inhaler. These medicines reduce swelling and irritation in the airways so that
breathing is easier over time. They control symptoms but do not cure asthma or COPD, and they
are not used to relieve a sudden attack.

## Dosage and administration

These medicines are breathed in through a metered-dose or dry-powder inhaler, usually once or
twice a day at about the same times each day, exactly as the clinician directs. They must be
used regularly to work, and it may take a week or longer before the full benefit is felt, so
keep using the inhaler even when feeling well. Do not use an inhaled corticosteroid to treat a
sudden asthma attack; a fast-acting rescue inhaler should be used for that instead. Rinsing the
mouth with water and spitting it out after each dose helps prevent mouth and throat side
effects, and the device should be used and cleaned according to its instructions.

## Warnings and precautions

Tell the clinician about pregnancy or breastfeeding, weak bones (osteoporosis), heart disease,
high blood pressure, eye problems such as glaucoma or cataracts, diabetes, liver disease, an
immune disorder, or any current or recent infection. People who have not had chickenpox or
measles, or the related vaccines, should avoid exposure to these illnesses, as inhaled steroids
can make infections harder to fight. Combination products should not be used together with
another long-acting beta-agonist, and the clinician should know about all other medicines,
including tricyclic antidepressants or MAO inhibitors used within the past two weeks. Do not stop
the medicine suddenly without medical advice, and report poor symptom control promptly.

## Adverse reactions

Common side effects can include hoarseness, throat irritation, cough, headache, runny or stuffy
nose, sore throat, nausea, or muscle aches, and these often improve over time. A white, patchy
mouth or throat infection (thrush) can occur and should be reported to the clinician. Get medical
care right away for trouble breathing or worsening wheezing that starts right after using the
inhaler, fast or pounding heartbeat, chest pain, fainting, vision changes, or signs of an allergic
reaction such as rash, hives, swelling of the face, lips, tongue, or throat, or trouble
swallowing; in children, slowed growth should be discussed with the clinician.
