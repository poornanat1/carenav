# Anemia Medications

## Indications and usage

Anemia medicines are used to treat or prevent anemia, a condition in which the body does not
have enough healthy red blood cells to carry oxygen. Iron supplements such as ferrous sulfate
treat iron-deficiency anemia and replace iron lost from bleeding, pregnancy, or a low-iron
diet. Vitamin B12 (cyanocobalamin) treats anemia caused by low B12 levels, including
pernicious anemia and poor absorption of the vitamin. Epoetin alfa, an
erythropoiesis-stimulating agent, prompts the bone marrow to make more red blood cells and is
used for anemia related to chronic kidney disease, certain cancer chemotherapy, or some other
conditions.

## Dosage and administration

Iron supplements are usually taken by mouth, often on an empty stomach for best absorption, or
with food if they upset the stomach; taking them with vitamin C may improve absorption. Vitamin
B12 may be given by mouth, by injection, or as a nasal spray, depending on the cause of the
deficiency, and pernicious anemia often requires lifelong treatment. Epoetin alfa is given by
injection under the skin or into a vein, usually by a healthcare provider or as directed.
Take the exact dose prescribed, keep all lab appointments, and do not change the dose on your
own, since blood counts and iron levels are monitored to guide treatment.

## Warnings and precautions

Tell your clinician about kidney disease, heart disease, high blood pressure, a history of
blood clots or stroke, cancer, pregnancy, and all medicines and supplements you take. Iron can
interact with antacids, certain antibiotics, and thyroid medicine, and very high doses of iron
are dangerous, especially to children, so keep supplements out of their reach. Epoetin alfa
carries warnings that it can raise the risk of blood clots, stroke, heart attack, and high
blood pressure, and in some cancers it may shorten survival or speed tumor growth, so it is
used at the lowest effective dose. Do not take more iron or B12 than directed, and report a
missed or worsening response to your clinician.

## Adverse reactions

Common side effects of iron include constipation, stomach upset, nausea, dark or black stools,
and stained teeth with liquid forms, while B12 may cause mild diarrhea or injection-site
discomfort. Epoetin alfa may cause high blood pressure, headache, joint or muscle aches, and
injection-site reactions. Get medical care right away for chest pain, shortness of breath,
trouble speaking, weakness or numbness on one side, leg pain or swelling, or signs of a severe
allergic reaction such as rash, swelling of the face or throat, or trouble breathing; with
iron overdose, seek emergency help immediately.
