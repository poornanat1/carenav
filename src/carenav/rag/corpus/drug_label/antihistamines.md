# Antihistamines and Allergy Medications

## Indications and usage

Antihistamines relieve allergy symptoms such as sneezing, runny nose, itchy or watery eyes,
and itching by blocking the action of histamine, a substance the body releases during an
allergic reaction. Newer, less sedating options such as cetirizine, loratadine, and
fexofenadine are commonly used for seasonal and year-round allergies and hives, while
diphenhydramine is an older antihistamine also used for allergy and cold symptoms, motion
sickness, and occasional sleeplessness. Epinephrine auto-injectors are a different kind of
allergy medicine used to treat severe, life-threatening allergic reactions (anaphylaxis), not
everyday allergy symptoms.

## Dosage and administration

Oral antihistamines come as tablets, chewable tablets, capsules, and liquids, and many are
sold without a prescription; follow the package label or your provider's directions and use a
proper measuring device, not a household spoon, for liquids. Diphenhydramine is usually taken
every four to six hours as needed, while cetirizine, loratadine, and fexofenadine are
typically taken once daily. An epinephrine auto-injector is given as a single injection into
the outer thigh at the first signs of a severe allergic reaction, and emergency care should be
called right away even after it is used because a second dose may be needed.

## Warnings and precautions

Diphenhydramine and other older antihistamines often cause drowsiness, so avoid driving or
operating machinery until you know how the medicine affects you, and do not combine them with
alcohol, which adds to the drowsiness. Older adults should generally avoid diphenhydramine
except to manage a serious allergic reaction, and people with lung disease, glaucoma, or
trouble urinating should use it with caution. Tell your provider or pharmacist about all
medicines, supplements, and herbal products you take, and about pregnancy or breastfeeding,
since dose adjustments may be needed; an epinephrine auto-injector should never be withheld in
a true anaphylaxis emergency even when other health conditions exist.

## Adverse reactions

Common side effects of antihistamines include dry mouth, nose, and throat, drowsiness,
dizziness, and, in children, unusual excitement, while less sedating products cause less
drowsiness; epinephrine may cause a fast or pounding heartbeat, sweating, shakiness, and
anxiety. Get medical care for vision changes or difficult or painful urination with
antihistamines, and call emergency services for any severe allergic reaction with trouble
breathing, swelling of the face or throat, or fainting, which calls for epinephrine right
away. If too much medicine is taken, contact Poison Control at 1-800-222-1222.
