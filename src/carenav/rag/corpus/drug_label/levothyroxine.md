# Levothyroxine

## Indications and usage

Levothyroxine is a synthetic thyroid hormone used to treat hypothyroidism, a condition in
which the thyroid gland does not make enough hormone. It is also used as part of treatment
for some thyroid conditions after surgery or radioactive iodine therapy. It works by
replacing thyroid hormone that the body normally produces, helping to relieve symptoms such
as fatigue, weight gain, and feeling cold.

## Dosage and administration

Levothyroxine is taken by mouth, usually once a day on an empty stomach, 30 minutes to 1
hour before breakfast, with a full glass of water. The dose usually starts low and is
adjusted by the clinician based on blood tests. Treatment controls but does not cure
hypothyroidism, so keep taking it even when you feel well, and do not stop without talking
to your clinician.

## Warnings and precautions

Levothyroxine should not be used to treat obesity or to cause weight loss. Tell your
clinician about heart disease, adrenal problems, or diabetes. Some medicines and
supplements—such as calcium, iron, and certain antacids—should be separated from your dose
by about four hours because they can reduce absorption. Tell your clinician about all
medicines you take.

## Adverse reactions

Taking the correct dose usually causes few side effects. Too much can cause symptoms like a
fast or irregular heartbeat, chest pain, tremor, nervousness, trouble sleeping, sweating,
and weight loss. Get medical attention right away for chest pain, rapid or irregular
heartbeat, or an allergic reaction such as rash, hives, or trouble breathing.
