# Blood Pressure Medications

## Indications and usage

Blood pressure medicines are used to treat high blood pressure (hypertension) when lifestyle
changes alone are not enough to control it, helping lower the risk of heart attack, stroke,
and kidney problems. Common classes include ACE inhibitors (such as enalapril and
benazepril) and ARBs (such as losartan, irbesartan, and valsartan), which relax blood
vessels; beta blockers (such as metoprolol, atenolol, carvedilol, and bisoprolol), which
make the heart beat slower and with less force; and calcium channel blockers (such as
amlodipine and verapamil), which relax blood vessels. Some of these medicines are also used
for heart failure, certain irregular heartbeats, angina, or after a heart attack, and
digoxin is used to treat heart failure and to slow a fast or irregular heartbeat.

## Dosage and administration

These medicines are usually taken by mouth once or twice a day, and many work best when
taken at the same time each day. Take the exact dose your clinician prescribes and do not
stop suddenly, especially with beta blockers, because stopping abruptly can worsen heart
problems. Keep taking the medicine even if you feel well, since high blood pressure often
has no symptoms, and have your blood pressure and bloodwork checked as directed. For
digoxin, the dose is carefully individualized and blood levels may be monitored.

## Warnings and precautions

Tell your clinician about kidney disease, liver disease, heart problems, diabetes, asthma,
pregnancy or plans to become pregnant, and all other medicines and supplements you take.
ACE inhibitors and ARBs can harm an unborn baby and should not be used during pregnancy, and
they can raise blood potassium, so use caution with potassium supplements or salt
substitutes. Beta blockers should be used carefully if you have asthma, slow heart rate, or
diabetes, and calcium channel blockers like verapamil can interact with many drugs,
including digoxin and grapefruit juice. Digoxin has a narrow safety margin and can become
toxic, especially with low potassium or certain other medicines, so report any changes
promptly.

## Adverse reactions

Common side effects depend on the class and may include dizziness, lightheadedness,
tiredness, headache, ankle or leg swelling, a dry cough (with ACE inhibitors), or a slow
heartbeat (with beta blockers). Get medical care right away for fainting, a very slow or
irregular heartbeat, chest pain, severe swelling, signs of high potassium such as muscle
weakness, or signs of digoxin toxicity such as nausea, vision changes, or confusion. Seek
emergency help for an allergic reaction such as swelling of the face, lips, tongue, or
throat, or trouble breathing.
