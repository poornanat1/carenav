# Anesthesia and Perioperative Medications

## Indications and usage

Anesthesia and perioperative medications are used during surgery and other medical procedures
to prevent pain, induce or maintain unconsciousness, and manage related symptoms. General
anesthetics such as propofol and inhaled agents like sevoflurane and isoflurane are used to
produce and maintain general anesthesia. Local anesthetics numb a specific area of the body so
a procedure can be done without pain. Ondansetron is used to prevent the nausea and vomiting
that can occur after surgery or anesthesia.

## Dosage and administration

These medicines are given by trained health professionals in a controlled clinical setting,
not self-administered. Propofol and ondansetron are usually given by injection into a vein, and
inhaled anesthetics like sevoflurane and isoflurane are delivered as a gas through a breathing
device. Local anesthetics are injected into or applied to the area being treated, and the dose
is individualized to the patient, the procedure, and the response. Patients are monitored
closely for breathing, heart rate, and blood pressure throughout administration and recovery.

## Warnings and precautions

These medicines should only be used where airway, breathing, and circulation can be monitored
and supported. Tell the care team about all medical conditions, allergies, and medicines being
taken, including a personal or family history of problems with anesthesia such as malignant
hyperthermia. Anesthetics can interact with sedatives, opioids, alcohol, and other
central nervous system depressants, increasing the risk of slowed breathing and low blood
pressure. Use is approached with extra caution in older adults, young children, pregnancy, and
people with heart, lung, liver, or kidney disease.

## Adverse reactions

Common side effects can include drowsiness, confusion, low blood pressure, nausea, vomiting,
shivering, sore throat, and pain or numbness at an injection or application site. Serious
reactions that require immediate medical care include trouble breathing, a very slow or
irregular heartbeat, severe allergic reaction with rash, swelling, or difficulty breathing,
seizures, and a high fever with muscle rigidity that may signal malignant hyperthermia. Most
side effects from short-term anesthesia resolve as the medicine wears off during recovery.
